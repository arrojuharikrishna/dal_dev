Credit and Lending

Probability of default (PD)

The likelihood that a borrower will fail to meet its financial obligations within a specified time horizon

"Data needed to calculate a Probability of Default (PD)

Borrower‑level financial information
Income statement items (revenue, EBITDA, operating profit).
Balance‑sheet metrics (total assets, liabilities, equity, leverage ratios).
Cash‑flow figures (operating cash flow, free cash flow, debt service coverage).
Liquidity measures (current ratio, quick ratio).
Credit history and behavior
Past payment performance (delinquencies, missed payments, restructuring).
Historical default events (date of default, severity).
Credit scores or internal rating grades.
Utilization of credit lines and any covenant breaches.
Qualitative/transactional data
Industry sector and sub‑industry classification.
Business model description and market position.
Management quality or governance assessments.
Collateral or guarantee details (if relevant to the rating).
Macroeconomic and market variables (often used to make the PD forward‑looking)
GDP growth, unemployment rates, inflation.
Interest‑rate levels, exchange‑rate movements.
Commodity price indices for sector‑specific exposure.
Modeling inputs / Features
Derived ratios (e.g., debt‑to‑EBITDA, interest‑coverage, profit‑margin).
Dummy variables for categorical factors (e.g., industry dummy, region dummy).
Lagged variables to capture trends over time.
Outcome variable for model training
Binary indicator of default (1 = default within the target horizon, 0 = non‑default).
Time‑to‑default information if using survival‑analysis techniques."

Business Impact:
Without PD:

Credit risk cannot be quantified
Lending decisions become subjective
Risk cannot be compared across borrowers
PD turns uncertainty into a measurable probability
